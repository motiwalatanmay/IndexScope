How this works
A walkthrough of every number in IndexScope — what the 4 cards mean, how the valuation bands are computed, and how to use the Backtest page to check whether history backs up today's signal. Five minutes, bottom to top.

1 · Quick start
IndexScope answers one question: are India's major equity indices cheap or expensive right now, relative to their own history? It doesn't pick stocks — it tells you whether the index as a whole is trading at an unusual valuation vs where it has historically traded.
Four indices are tracked: Nifty 50, Nifty Next 50, Nifty Midcap 150, Nifty Smallcap 250. Each gets a card on the Dashboard. Green = cheap vs history. Yellow = neutral. Red = expensive.
Three-step workflow
1
Scan the Dashboard. The 4 cards give an at-a-glance view. Card color = valuation band. A green Smallcap 250 card with a red Nifty 50 card tells you midcap/smallcap is cheap relative to large-cap right now.
2
Click any card for details. Opens a full-screen view with three sub-tabs: Details (P/E + P/B + Dividend Yield + EPS + sparkline), Historical Medians (how the medians themselves have drifted over time), and Backtest.
3
Verify on Backtest. Every past date when this index was at today's valuation level — what happened to prices over the next 6 months and 1 year? Run the backtest to see if the signal has historically worked.
Before you use these signals
Cheap can get cheaper. A "Very Cheap" band tells you an index is unusually depressed vs its own history. It is not a prediction of when it will recover. These signals work best for patient investors with a 6–12 month horizon, not for next-week timing.

3 · Valuation bands
The band is determined by a single ratio: current P/E ÷ weighted historical reference. That ratio maps to one of five bands:
Very Cheap
≤ 80% of ref
Cheap
80 – 90% of ref
Neutral
90 – 105% of ref
Expensive
105 – 120% of ref
Very Expensive
> 120% of ref
Why the Neutral band is asymmetric
The Neutral band extends slightly further above median (up to 105%) than below (90%). Indices naturally drift slightly above their own historical median over long periods as nominal earnings grow with inflation and the investor base expands. Being 5% above median is not yet a warning.
Secondary signal: percentile rank
Each metric card also shows a 5-year percentile bar. This measures frequency rather than magnitude — "how often did this index trade this cheap in the last 5 years?". A 15th-percentile reading means the index is cheaper than 85% of the last 5 years of daily observations.
The percentile is winsorised — outliers are clipped at the 1st and 99th percentile before ranking. This prevents the COVID P/E spike (which briefly exceeded 800× for the Midcap index as earnings collapsed) from dominating the rank.
Divergence flag
If the primary signal (% of ref) and the secondary signal (percentile) disagree — e.g. an index is Cheap vs its anchor but has spent half the last 5 years at this level — a small amber ⚠ capsule appears under the metric card. Interpret this as: "the market thinks this is a normal level, even if it looks cheap by historical standards."
4 · Weighted reference
Rather than compare today to a single median, IndexScope blends three historical windows so the anchor adapts to regime changes without over-reacting to them.
The weighting formula
3-year median
× 30%
5-year median
× 60%
10-year median
× 10%
// Weighted reference PE ref = 0.30 × median(last 3yr) + 0.60 × median(last 5yr) + 0.10 × median(last 10yr) // Band determined by the ratio pct = (current PE ÷ ref) × 100 // If any window is unavailable (e.g. Smallcap 250 data // starts in 2016, so the 10yr window is partial), // weights redistribute proportionally.
Why 60% weight on the 5-year median?
The 5-year window covers roughly one full business cycle in India — long enough to include both peaks and troughs, short enough to reflect today's regime. The 3-year window adds sensitivity to recent rate cycles. The 10-year leg gets only 10% weight so that indices which have permanently re-rated (like Smallcap 250 post-2020) aren't forever compared against a baseline that no longer applies.
Worked example — Nifty 50
Suppose today's Nifty 50 P/E is 22.8 and the three medians are:
3yr median
23.5 → × 30% = 7.05
5yr median
24.2 → × 60% = 14.52
10yr median
22.1 → × 10% = 2.21
Weighted ref
23.78
Ratio
22.8 ÷ 23.78 = 96% → Neutral
If the P/E dropped to 20.0 instead, the ratio would be 20.0 ÷ 23.78 = 84% → Cheap.
5 · Details view
Clicking any card on the Dashboard opens the Details view. It fills the screen with a back button in the top-left, the index name, and a sub-nav of three tabs.

Details sub-tab
Three metric cards side by side: Price / Earnings, Price / Book, Dividend Yield. Each shows today's value, the band pill, a 3-month trend badge, a median comparison table (3yr / 5yr / 10yr), and a 5-year percentile bar.
Below that: the Derived EPS section (EPS = Index Value ÷ P/E) with YoY growth and "% of peak", followed by a 3-year P/E sparkline with the weighted reference overlaid as a dashed line.
Trend badge
Each metric card shows a small trend badge like ↗ +0.8 vs 3M ago or ↘ −1.2 vs 3M ago. This compares the current P/E to its value 63 trading days (~3 months) earlier and helps identify whether valuation pressure is rising or easing.
Historical Medians sub-tab
This tab shows how the rolling medians themselves have changed over time — letting you spot structural re-rating. If the 5yr median P/E has risen from 18× (2019) to 24× (2024), paying 23× today may not be as expensive as it appears on a long-horizon basis.
Annual frequency shows year-end snapshots. Quarterly shows end-of-quarter values for finer resolution.
EPS Turning flag
On the Details sub-tab, a small amber ▲ TURNING badge appears under the YoY EPS value when EPS has been rising over both the 90-day and 180-day windows but year-over-year growth is still modest (≤5%). It's an early-cycle inflection signal — earnings are turning up before the annual numbers show it clearly.
6 · Backtest (Relative vs Absolute)
The Backtest tab answers: every time this index was at this valuation level in the past, what happened to prices next? IndexScope merges two different backtest modes behind a single toggle at the top of the page:
Relative Absolute
Relative mode
Fires a signal when current P/E is within ±1% of a chosen % of the rolling weighted reference. You set the threshold as a percentage — e.g. 85% means "fire a signal every past date where PE was 85% of its weighted reference." The reference is recomputed at every historical date, so the threshold adapts to regime shifts automatically.
When to use: Most of the time. This is the primary mode. It answers "was today's cheapness historically significant?" — the threshold you pick should roughly equal the % of ref number shown on the Dashboard card.
Absolute mode
Fires a signal when current P/E falls below a fixed number (e.g. "PE < 25"). No reference, no ratio — just a hard threshold on the raw P/E value. After each signal, buy at close and sell after a fixed hold period (3/6/12/24 months).
When to use: For simple rule-based strategies ("buy Nifty whenever PE drops below 20"). The downside: PE 25 meant very different things in 2016 vs 2024, so absolute thresholds don't adapt to regime changes. Relative mode usually gives more robust signals.
How to read the results
After you click Run, a summary row appears showing total signals, hit rate (% of signals with positive 1yr return), average 6-month and 1-year CAGR, and the buy-and-hold CAGR of just holding the index over the same period as a benchmark.
The table below shows every individual signal, colored by outcome:
2020-03-24 PE: 18.2 · 74% of ref +38.2% 1yr CAGR Hit
2022-01-17 PE: 28.4 · 114% of ref −4.1% 1yr CAGR Miss
2025-11-08 PE: 22.1 · 86% of ref — not yet 1yr Open
Green rows = positive 1-year return at that signal. Red = negative. Yellow = signal fired too recently for a 1-year forward return to be computed.
Cooldown slider
Controls the minimum gap between consecutive signals. Default 90 days. Without a cooldown, one multi-week dip below threshold would fire dozens of near-duplicate signals and inflate your sample with correlated trades. Rule of thumb: set cooldown ≥ your intended holding period.
Interpretation guide
  • Hit rate > 70%, avg 1yr CAGR clearly positive → strong setup, proceed with conviction.
  • High average but low hit rate → noisy signal, a few huge wins masking many losses. Fragile.
  • Mostly red rows, poor hit rate → broken setup, either wrong threshold or signals cluster around earnings collapses.
  • Too few signals (< 5) → not a pattern, just a handful of data points. Widen the threshold or extend the date range.
7 · Limits & caveats
This tool is designed to sharpen decisions, not replace judgement. The honest limits:
  • Valuation is not timing. A "Very Cheap" signal can stay cheap — or get cheaper — for months. These are 6–12 month signals, not next-week calls.
  • Index composition changes over time. Nifty indices reconstitute periodically (quarterly for Midcap 150, for instance). The 10-year P/E series blends companies that were in the index at different dates. Treat long histories as approximate, not precise.
  • COVID outlier handling. The April-2020 earnings collapse produced P/E prints that exceeded 800× for Midcap 150. These are winsorised at the 99th percentile in percentile-rank calculations but still distort simple historical medians. The weighted reference is less affected because the 3/5/10yr medians are each individually resistant to a single outlier quarter.
  • No macro overlay. The model knows nothing about rate cycles, the INR, global commodity prices, or credit conditions. A "Cheap" signal during a credit crisis is a different risk than during stable macro. Add your own read.
  • EPS is derived, not reported. Nifty doesn't publish a single index-level EPS, so IndexScope derives it as Index Value ÷ P/E. One-off accounting items at a constituent level can briefly distort it. Cross-check with reported quarterly results if EPS looks surprising.
  • Structural regime changes. The 10% weight on the 10-year median means the model adapts gradually to re-ratings. During a transition (e.g. Smallcap 250 post-2020), treat signals as directional rather than precise.
Best practice
Use this as one structured input among several. The highest-conviction setups are when: (1) the band is Cheap or Very Cheap, (2) the EPS panel shows ▲ TURNING or healthy YoY growth, (3) the backtest at this threshold shows a strong hit rate, and (4) your own fundamental/macro view doesn't contradict the signal. All four together is a strong setup.
